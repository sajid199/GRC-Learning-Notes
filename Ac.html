What is Risk?

Understanding Risk

Definition

A risk is anything that can negatively impact your business — either financially (loss of money, fraud) or reputationally (loss of trust, audit findings).

Probability

Risk is measured by how likely it is to happen. Probability depends on experience — your own or someone else's.

Example: Betting on a coin flip = 50% probability of losing.

Risk is NOT Universal

Risk depends on local factors — it varies from company to company and country to country. A risk relevant to a bank may not apply to a retail company.

Risk Lifecycle

What Do You Do with a Risk?

1

Risk Identification — Risk Analysis

Find out what risks exist in your system. Run access risk analysis to detect them.

2

Risk Remediation — Remove or Avoid

Take the conflicting access away from the user. Fix the root cause so the risk no longer exists.

3

Risk Mitigation — Reduce the Impact

If you cannot remove the access, put controls in place (manual checks, approvals, monitoring) to reduce the damage if the risk occurs.

Risk Levels

Impact-Based Classification

Every risk in GRC is rated based on how badly it could hurt the business.

LOW
Minor impact. Unlikely to cause financial loss. Still flagged for awareness.
MEDIUM
Moderate impact. Could cause issues if exploited but not immediately dangerous.
HIGH
Significant impact. Likely to cause financial loss or audit failure.
CRITICAL
Severe impact. Can cause major fraud, regulatory breach, or reputational damage.
Segregation of Duties

SOD Risk Explained

What is an SOD Risk?

SOD = Segregation of Duties. It means certain tasks should be done by different people, not one person alone. When one user has access to both, a risk (the opportunity for fraud or error) exists.

Classic Example: The person who receives payment (T1) should NOT also be the person who can adjust payments (T2). If one user has both → SOD Risk.

Two Analysis Levels

Where Can SOD be Detected?

Action Level
Does the user have both T-codes? Checks at the transaction level regardless of auth values.
T1 + T2 exists
Permission Level
Does the user have both T-codes AND the auth values that allow real execution?
T1 (Full)T2 (Full)

Permission Level Combinations (for Action Level)

At action level, a risk is checked across all combinations of Display (D) and Full (F) access:

T1D + T2D T1F + T2D T1D + T2F T1F + T2F

D = Display only  |  F = Full / Change access
T1F + T2F is the highest-risk combination (full access to both conflicting actions).

Global Rule Set

Where Do Risks Come From?

How Was the Global Rule Set Created?

SAP studied large, reputed companies across industries — Banking, Automobile, Retail, Manufacturing etc. — and collected the risks those companies actually faced over decades.

These risks were documented, categorized by business process (Finance, HR, Sales, Procurement…), and packaged as the Global Rule Set.

In short: Global Rule Set = 100+ years of real-world business risk experience, packed into GRC, ready to use.

How Is It Loaded?

The Global Rule Set data is loaded into your GRC system using BC Sets (Business Configuration Sets) related to RULESET activation. This is done during implementation.

Custom Rule Set

Creating Your Own Rule Set

Not every risk in the Global Rule Set applies to your company. You may also have risks specific to your business. For this, you create a Custom Rule Set.

1

Create the Rule Set

Define the container that will hold your custom risks.

NWBC → Setup → Access Rule Maintenance → Rule Set
2

Define Business Processes

Categorize risks by business process (Finance, HR, Sales…) in SPRO.

SPRO → GRC → Access Control → Maintain Business Process and Sub Processes
3

Create Functions

A Function = a business activity that groups related T-codes. You need at least two conflicting functions to define an SOD risk.

NWBC → Setup → Access Rule Maintenance → Functions
4

Define Risks

Combine two conflicting functions into a Risk. Assign risk type and level. Assign to your rule set.

NWBC → Setup → Access Rule Maintenance → Risks
Classification

Three Types of Risk in GRC

1. SOD Risk — Segregation of Duties

Occurs when one user has access to two conflicting functions. These are things that should always be done by separate people.

SU01 + PFCG SCC4 + STMS

SU01 = Create users, PFCG = Maintain roles → One person doing both = access management risk.
SCC4 = Manage clients, STMS = Transport management → One person doing both = change management risk.

2. Critical Action Risk

A single T-code that is so powerful it is dangerous on its own — regardless of who else has it. Just having it is the risk.

SCC4 SCC5 SE38 SPRO

SE38 = ABAP editor (can run or modify programs)  |  SCC4 = Client settings (can open/lock clients)

3. Critical Permission Risk

Not just having the T-code, but having it with specific high-risk authorization values. This is more granular than Critical Action.

T-codeAuth ObjectRisky Activity ValuesWhy Risky
SCC4 S_TABU_DIS Activity 01 (Create) or 02 (Change) Can modify client-level system tables
SE38 S_DEVELOP Activity 01 (Create) or 02 (Change) Can write or modify ABAP programs
Key rule: Wherever you see a T-code in a risk definition, treat it as an Action. Wherever you see an authorization object + field values, treat it as a Permission.
Interview Prep

Common Questions & Model Answers

Click each question to expand a clear, concise answer you can say naturally in an interview.

Q
Are you using Global Rule Sets or Custom Rule Sets in your company?
▾
Model Answer:

"In our project, we started with the Global Rule Set provided by SAP as the baseline — it covers standard industry risks across Finance, HR, and Procurement processes. However, we also created Custom Rule Sets to address risks that are specific to our business environment. For example, some T-codes in our custom ERP landscape are not covered by the Global Rule Set, so we defined custom functions and risks for those. In most real implementations, you use a mix of both."
  • If you have only used Global — be honest and say so, but mention you understand how to create custom ones.
  • Global Rule Set is loaded via BC Sets during implementation.
Q
Have you created a custom risk? Walk me through the steps.
▾
Model Answer:

"Yes. The steps to create a custom risk are:
  • Step 1 — Rule Set: Create a rule set in NWBC → Setup → Access Rule Maintenance → Rule Set. This is the container.
  • Step 2 — Business Process: Define or reuse a business process via SPRO → GRC → Access Control → Maintain Business Process and Sub Processes.
  • Step 3 — Functions: Create two functions (e.g., Payment Processing and Payment Adjustment) and assign the relevant T-codes to each function.
  • Step 4 — Risk: Create a risk that links the two conflicting functions, assign a risk type (SOD/Critical Action/Critical Permission) and a risk level (Low/Medium/High/Critical), and map it to the rule set.
After this, the risk is ready to be used in access risk analysis runs."
Q
What is the difference between Action Level and Permission Level analysis?
▾
Model Answer:

"Action Level checks whether a user has both T-codes assigned — it doesn't look at the authorization values inside. It tells you the T-code combination exists.

Permission Level goes deeper — it checks whether the user has the T-code and the authorization values that allow them to actually execute the conflicting activity. It is more accurate but heavier to run.

In practice, Action Level gives quick results; Permission Level is used when you need precise, audit-quality reports."
Q
What are the three types of risk in SAP GRC Access Control?
▾
Model Answer:

  • SOD Risk: Two conflicting functions assigned to the same user. E.g., User Creation (SU01) + Role Maintenance (PFCG). The risk is that one person can create a user and give them any roles they want.
  • Critical Action Risk: A single T-code that is risky by itself. E.g., SE38 (ABAP editor) — anyone with this can write or modify programs, which is dangerous in a production system.
  • Critical Permission Risk: A T-code combined with specific authorization values. E.g., SE38 + S_DEVELOP with Activity 01 or 02 — more precise than just having SE38.
Q
What is the difference between Risk Remediation and Risk Mitigation?
▾
Model Answer:

"Remediation means you remove the risk. You take away the conflicting access — the risk no longer exists in the system.

Mitigation means you cannot remove the access (maybe the business needs it), so instead you put manual controls in place — like a manager reviewing the activity every month, or a monitoring job — to reduce the impact if something goes wrong.

Remediation is always preferred. Mitigation is the fallback when remediation is not possible."
Q
What is the Global Rule Set and how is it loaded?
▾
Model Answer:

"The Global Rule Set is a pre-built collection of SOD and critical access risks that SAP has compiled by studying large, reputed companies across industries — banking, retail, manufacturing, etc. — over many decades. It covers standard business processes like Finance, HR, Sales, and Procurement.

It is loaded into your GRC system using BC Sets (Business Configuration Sets) related to RULESET activation. This is typically done during the initial implementation or system setup."
